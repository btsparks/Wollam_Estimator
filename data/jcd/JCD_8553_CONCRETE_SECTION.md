## 2. SCOPE SUMMARY

Major structural concrete program for a below-grade pump station serving the Slice II Tailings Single Point Discharge system. The structure functions like a basement excavated into the tailings impoundment embankment. Total concrete volume approximately 10,000+ CY placed.

**Primary Structural Elements:**
- **Mat Slab (CMS_)**: 5'-6" thick reinforced mat foundation, approximately 220' x 130' footprint, with two integrated floor sumps. Bottom of foundation at EL 90'-2", top of mat at EL 95'-8".
- **Octagon Tank (CO_)**: Central octagonal slurry collection tank with ~30' tall walls (same height as perimeter walls). This is where tailings slurry is collected before pumping. Connected to the inlet structure via concrete channel.
- **Perimeter Walls (CW_)**: 24-29' tall reinforced concrete walls with integrated counterforts (buttresses) at regular spacing. Shear keys at mat-to-wall construction joints. Waterstop throughout.
- **Inlet Structure (DS_)**: Concrete intake channel/tunnel connecting the 63" HDPE tailings delivery pipeline to the octagon tank. Includes overflow connection to overflow ponds via separate 63" discharge.
- **Loading Dock (CLD_)**: Elevated platform section providing at-grade truck access to the below-grade structure. Void beneath the dock filled with geofoam (~$489K in foam purchased).
- **Pipe Supports (CPS_)**: Numerous isolated concrete pedestals for piping throughout the facility.
- **Equipment Pads (CEQ_)**: Foundation pads for SPD pumps, PDC, and various mechanical equipment with anchor bolt installations.

## 4. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Formwork & Methods

**Mat Slab Forming:**
- Edge forms using Forshor Alisply panel system, engineer-stamped for 11" per hour pour rate
- Stay-form construction joints with waterstop at all pour breaks
- Form savers installed at wall-to-mat joints for rebar continuity
- Company-owned and rented forms — Form Rental actual: $179K vs. $370K budget (significant savings)

**Wall Forming:**
- Gang forms built on-site from Forshor wall form package
- Typical gang panels ranged 325-425 SF each
- Wall forms crane-set after assembly
- Wall curtains (rebar cages) pre-tied by Iron Mountain and crane-set into place
- Buttress/counterfort forms integrated with wall pours
- One form blow-out incident during wall pour (contained, minor impact)

**Octagon Forming:**
- Special octagon form panels — not radius forms, but angled flat panels forming octagonal geometry
- Forms delivered separately from wall form package
- Form savers in columns connecting to octagon walls
- Complex geometry required careful sequencing

### Pour Strategy — Critical Decision

**Design vs. Execution:**
The design drawings showed many optional construction joints throughout the mat slab. The RFP stated: *"Construction joints in the mat foundation are optional and may be skipped if continuous supply of concrete can be maintained."*

**Wollam's Approach:** Instead of 5-7+ smaller pours as shown on drawings, executed **3 massive continuous pours** for the mat slab:
- Pour 1: ~1,570 CY (January 19, 2024)
- Pour 2: ~1,630 CY (February 1, 2024)  
- Pour 3: ~2,940 CY (largest pour)

Used only the long east-west construction joint and center north-south joint. Had to be mindful of joint locations to avoid splitting pump pads and pipe support pedestals.

**Benefits:**
- Fewer cold joints = better waterproofing
- Reduced mobilization/demobilization cycles
- Saved significant cost vs. bid assumption (bid planned for more than 3 pours)
- Offset the higher-than-expected escort costs

**Challenges:**
- Massive coordination — 60+ crew members on major pours
- 18-hour continuous placement requiring 2 batch plants
- Night pours (started at midnight) due to site logistics
- Equipment failures under stress (65-meter pump broke down at end of Pour 2, finished last 1/4 CY with buckets)

### Concrete Escort Operations

**Challenge:** The pump station sits on top of the active tailings impoundment. Night pours were necessary. Rio Tinto required:
- Dedicated gate guards at entry points
- Individual escorts for every concrete truck from gate to pour location
- This was an **unknown requirement at bid time**

**Budget Impact:**
- Budget: $33,119 ($2.63/CY)
- Actual: $73,154 ($6.49/CY) — **2.5x over budget**

**Mitigation:** By reducing the number of pours from the bid assumption (more than 3) to the actual 3 massive pours, Wollam saved enough on pump costs, form cycles, and crew mobilization to absorb the escort cost overrun. The job overall remained well under budget.

### Cold Weather Concrete

**Scope Increase:** 115 days of cold weather protection vs. 66 days budgeted (74% more winter days than planned)

**Protection Measures:**
- Heated enclosures over active pour areas
- Concrete blankets for cure protection
- Ground heaters (Thawzall units) for subgrade prep
- Glycol heaters for maintaining cure temps
- Weekend heater monitoring (Randy Evans checking/fueling heaters on Saturdays/Sundays)

**Cost Management:** Despite nearly double the cold weather days, cost per day was managed well:
- Budget: $2,573/day
- Actual: $1,358/day
- Net result: $125K actual vs. $170K budget (under budget despite more days)

### Lessons Learned — Concrete

**What Worked Well:**
1. **Large continuous pours** — Skipping optional construction joints saved substantial cost and improved quality. Budget assumed more pours; executing 3 large pours instead saved on pumping, forming, and mobilization.
2. **Gang form strategy** — Building gang forms on-site allowed customization to the wall geometry and achieved consistent 0.28 MH/SF rate across 36,000 SF.
3. **Pre-tied wall curtains** — Having Iron Mountain pre-tie wall curtains and crane-setting them into place streamlined the forming-to-pour cycle.
4. **Cold weather cost management** — Despite 74% more winter days than planned, managed to stay under cold weather budget through efficient daily operations.
5. **TLM finishing crews** — Hiring specialized concrete finishing subcontractor was essential for achieving quality on 18-hour continuous pours.

**What Would We Do Differently:**
1. **Escort costs** — The RTK escort requirement was a significant unknown. For future mine site work, explicitly clarify traffic control and escort requirements during bid phase. Don't assume logical solutions (marked routes) will be accepted.
2. **Equipment pad complexity** — The 48% overrun on CEQ_F/S Pads suggests the estimate didn't fully account for anchor bolt and embed density on mechanical pads. For pump stations, add complexity factor to equipment pad forming.
3. **Pump redundancy** — The 65-meter pump breakdown at end of Pour 2 was a close call. For massive continuous pours, ensure backup pump or crane/bucket contingency is explicitly planned.
