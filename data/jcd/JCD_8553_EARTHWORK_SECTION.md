## 2. KEY VALUE ENGINEERING DECISION

### Excavation Slope Change: 4:1 → 2:1

**Bid Assumption:** 4:1 excavation slopes due to perceived instability of tailings material.

**Actual Execution:** Hired Sunrise Engineering to perform geotechnical analysis. Site had been under surcharge for 2+ years prior to construction, providing significant consolidation. Sunrise approved 2:1 excavation slopes.

**Impact:**
- Structural fill quantity remained unchanged (fixed envelope around foundation walls)
- Whole tailings backfill quantity significantly reduced
- VE credit issued to RTK for reduced backfill volume
- Reduced excavation footprint improved site access and logistics

**Lesson Learned:** For tailings impoundment work, always request geotechnical analysis early. Historical surcharge data can justify steeper slopes and significant cost savings.

## 11. LESSONS LEARNED

### What Worked Well

1. **Geotechnical analysis paid off** — 2:1 vs 4:1 slope change saved significant backfill cost and earned VE credit

2. **Short haul + 4 trucks = high production** — Even at 1,200' cycle, the 4th truck prevented excavator waiting

3. **Two excavators better than one** — PC650 + PC490 allowed continuous production, operator relief, equipment maintenance flexibility

4. **Full-time floor dozer is mandatory** — Multiple foreman notes confirm trucks couldn't run efficiently without continuous floor maintenance

5. **Tailings material handled well** — No rock, consistent loading, even performed in rain ("material handled rain well, 283 loads")

6. **Work plan held up** — Field adjustments were additive (more trucks, extra dozer) not fundamental changes to approach

7. **Wheel rolling adequate for whole tailings** — Achieved compaction spec primarily through haul truck traffic, minimized roller time

### What to Do Differently

1. **Clarify vertical drainage scope early** — DDS aggregate work was descoped; better to identify during bid if quantities are uncertain

2. **Budget for grader in haul operations** — Cat 16M wasn't in original work plan but was essential; include in standard earthwork spread

3. **Plan for cell signal issues** — CB radios were necessary for truck communication in excavation; anticipate on remote/industrial sites

4. **Verify structural fill source availability** — Started with Geneva at $15.25/ton, had to switch to Rhine at $14.14/ton; confirm supplier capacity and pricing before finalizing bid

5. **Track excavation destination split** — Knowing how much went to stockpile vs. dike vs. offhaul helps verify earthwork balance; improve daily tracking

### Estimating Guidance for Similar Work

**Tailings excavation on Kennecott impoundment:**
- Material is easy — budget for good production
- Full-time floor maintenance is non-negotiable
- Short haul (<1,500') favors more trucks over bigger excavator
- Request geotech early for slope optimization
- Anticipate 6,000-7,000 CY/day with proper spread

**Structural backfill around below-grade structures:**
- Special compaction (5' zone, 4" lifts) adds significant time near walls
- Coordinate with concrete operations — crane positioning affects fill sequence
- On-road delivery trucks need escorts on RTK property — budget accordingly
- Rhine pit at $14.14/ton delivered is current benchmark
